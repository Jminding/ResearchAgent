Summary

This paper investigates whether RL-based GNN decoders for surface-code QEC scale effectively to practical code distances (‚â•15). The authors report a strong performance advantage over MWPM at small distances (d ‚â§ 7) but a reversal at d = 15, where RL performs worse than MWPM. The paper frames this as a meaningful negative result and proposes diagnostic hypotheses and follow-up experiments.

The problem is timely and relevant, the writing is exceptionally structured, and the paper demonstrates admirable methodological self-awareness. However, the empirical evidence supporting the main claim is limited by undertraining, small sample sizes, and incomplete ablation studies. As written, the findings are suggestive rather than conclusive.

Strengths

Valuable diagnostic perspective

Weaknesses / Concerns

Insufficient empirical support for the scaling claim

n = 2 seeds

~200 training episodes despite acknowledging theoretical requirements of 10‚Å∑‚Äì10‚Å∏

Large variance and unstable convergence

Under these conditions, the failure mode may simply reflect incomplete optimization rather than fundamental non-scalability.

Undertraining is hypothesized but not tested
The paper argues undertraining is the likely cause of failure, yet does not conduct:

extended-training experiments

learning-curve analysis to show saturation

even a small-scale ablation

This weakens the causal interpretation.

Over-interpretation of zero-shot distance generalization results

Absolute error rate mismatch raises validity concerns

possible simulator or configuration errors, or

operation above threshold
